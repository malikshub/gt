What is ISO?

by Shubham Malik April 29th, 2021

What is ISO?

Did you ever feel that your photo in any dark environment is more exposed and distorted than usual, that is because of the high ISO. ISO is one of the most common environments people typically miss out on. ISO in photography refers to two features of the photo one that's exposure and other clarity or grain amount.

In more common words, ISO is a setting in a camera that deals with brightness or darkness, and clarity and distortion. ISO typically ranges from ISO 100, ISO 200, ISO 400 to ISO 6400 (in general), and so on. ISO 100 is considered as Low ISO and ISO 6400 and above is considered as High ISO.

Which ISO value should you prefer?

ISO value selection is simply based on the environment you are shooting in, It can be said if you are shooting in a bright and sunny environment then use ISO 100/200 which will block excess exposure as well as produce a higher quality image with the minimum amount of grain.

If you are shooting in an artificial lighting environment where you don't have enough light then you must use it somewhere around ISO 400 to balance the amount of brightness, the picture produced with this setting might not be the best in quality but at least it would have enough amount of exposure in the image.

And if you are shooting in extremely low light conditions then you might require high ISO such as 6400 or above if your camera supports above 6400. Using these ISO value will make your image distorted with higher grain amount, which could be fixed in post-production thou'.

What do I suggest?

As we know using Low ISO settings you can get high-quality images but using Low ISO settings might not be ideal for every condition and on the other hand using High ISO in low light condition might be right but in turn, will make the image quality low and increases the grain amount.

So, I would suggest you first get some sample shots using different ISO settings, then based on those shots choose the settings which you feel would be ideal. Also, you can choose to use Low ISO even in low light if you feel that you can fix the exposure in post-production, and if you are good at fixing grain amount then you can choose to shoot in High ISO settings.

That been said, you can choose to fix the damage being done by High ISO settings or simply choose to use Low ISO and fix exposure in post-production if required.
